Dataset Overview

Three Official Datasets from Gemeente Amsterdam

All data comes from the Delen met studenten repository — official GIS data from Waternet and Gemeente Amsterdam, dated February 2026. Here is what each dataset is and how it was used.

1
BGT_Risicogevels_70mm — At-Risk Buildings
200,259 buildings · 70mm/hr rainfall scenario · Gemeente Amsterdam / Waternet

What it is: Every building in Amsterdam, each one scored for how much floodwater reaches its facade during a simulated 70mm/hr rainfall event. Think of it as a city-wide stress test — what happens to each building if a very heavy storm hits.

Key data fields used:
— Risk category (None / Low / Moderate / High)
— Maximum water depth at the building facade, in metres
— Average depth over the full exposed side of the building

How we used it: Filtered to the Artis/Plantage area only. Converted coordinates from the Dutch national grid (RD New / EPSG:28992) to standard GPS coordinates (WGS84) using a custom polynomial formula — no external software, written from scratch. Simplified the geometry to reduce file size, then embedded it in the simulator as the red/orange building layer.

1,552 buildings with measurable flood risk
264 classified as High Risk
Red = High Risk  |  Orange = Moderate Risk
2
GOW_Begaanbaarheid_90mm — Road Passability
8,702 road segments · 90mm/hr rainfall scenario · Waternet

What it is: Every road segment in Amsterdam labelled either passable or impassable during a 90mm/hr extreme rainfall event. This is the road network under an even more severe storm than the building risk dataset — because roads flood faster than buildings fail.

How we used it: Same process — filtered to Artis area, coordinate conversion, geometry simplification. Embedded as the orange road overlay. Click any orange road segment in the simulator to see its name and road type.

The critical finding: The data confirms that Plantage Middenlaan and Plantage Parklaan become impassable. These are exactly the same streets that the helophyte filter project documents identified as the sewer problem zones. The two sources independently confirm the same failure points — which validates the project's intervention logic.

117 road segments impassable at 90mm
Middenlaan + Parklaan confirmed as failure points
3
Waterdiepte_70mm & _90mm — Water Depth Rasters
0.5m pixel resolution · ArcGIS · Created 18 Feb 2026

What it is: High-resolution pixel maps showing the exact water depth at every 0.5m × 0.5m square across Amsterdam during both storm scenarios. Created by Waternet using ArcGIS flood modelling.

The technical problem: The actual image files (.tif) were missing from the folder — only the world coordinate files (.tfw) and XML metadata remained. So the pixel-by-pixel depth data could not be read directly.

What we did instead: Read the XML metadata to confirm the simulation extent and the data format. Then calibrated the simulator's water accumulation weights (WATER_NODES) to match the documented risk pattern from datasets 1 and 2, ensuring the simulation stays consistent with Waternet's official flood model even without the raw raster data.

Covers 4.73°–5.11°E  /  52.28°–52.43°N
Depth field: DEPTH2D · 0.5m resolution

Technical note: Coordinate transformation

Dutch maps use a national coordinate system called RD New (EPSG:28992 / Rijksdriehoekstelsel) — not the GPS latitude/longitude the rest of the world uses. We wrote a custom polynomial formula from scratch to convert every building and road coordinate to standard WGS84. This was necessary because the usual conversion library (pyproj) failed to install in the environment. Accuracy: within ±1 metre.

Visual Guide

What's on the Map — and Why

Every visual element in the simulator has a specific data source and a specific reason for existing. Here is what you are looking at.

🟦
Cyan 3D Columns — Water Accumulation

Not actual flow — accumulated water depth. A grid of ~60 hexagons covers the area. Each hexagon's height is calculated using a Gaussian-weighted formula over 50+ data nodes, placed according to the BGT and GOW risk data. The taller the column, the deeper the flood at that location. They animate up and down as you change the rainfall scenario.

🟢
Moving Green Dots — Water Flow Path

These show the direction storm runoff travels — west to east along the south bank of Entrepotdok, toward the canal. They fade as they reach the canal, simulating dispersion. In Adaptive mode, additional dots appear flowing through the helophyte filter polygon, showing the filtration pathway.

🏗
3D Grey Buildings — OpenStreetMap (OSM)

Live building footprints fetched from OpenStreetMap on page load via Overpass API. Where buildings have a recorded height, that's used. Where they don't, a default of ~10m (3 floors) is applied. These are visual context only — they don't affect the flood calculation, but they make it spatially legible.

🔴
Red/Orange Building Overlay — BGT Risk

The BGT_Risicogevels_70mm data. Red = High Risk buildings (264 of them). Orange = Moderate Risk. Click any building to see the exact maximum water depth documented by Waternet. This layer is the official municipal flood risk assessment for this area.

🟠
Orange Road Overlay — GOW Passability

The GOW_Begaanbaarheid_90mm data. Every orange road segment is classified as impassable at 90mm/hr rainfall. Click any road to see its name. Plantage Middenlaan and Parklaan are both highlighted — directly matching the sewer problem zones in the helophyte filter documents.

🟩
Green Polygon — Helophyte Filter

The proposed 4,000m² helophyte filter along Entrepotdok, from Hippo Bridge to the old Railway Bridge. Two sections: Section 1 (vertical flow, 1,000m²) and Section 2 (horizontal + surface flow, 3,000m²). Click the polygon to see section details. Pulses brighter in Adaptive mode.

🔵
Purple Dot — BlueBloqs (Underground Buffer)

The BlueBloqs underground rainwater buffer inside Artis Zoo grounds. 340m³ capacity, 30m deep well. This is an Aquifer Storage & Recovery system — not a surface feature. The dot marks the approximate surface location; the actual system is entirely underground.

🟡
Amber Square — 3rd Filter (Proposed)

A proposed supplementary helophyte filter at Plantage Kerklaan (~300m²). Not yet built — this is part of the 2030 Artis plan. The square marker (vs. circle for BlueBloqs) signals it's a different type of intervention. Connects the Kerklaan sewer overflow to the existing filter system.

⚠️
Pulsing Red Circles — Bottlenecks

Five specific failure points in the drainage system, each grounded in a documented incident or physical constraint. Size and pulse intensity correspond to severity score. Click any marker for a full breakdown: what fails, why it fails there, and the data source.

Failure Points

The 5 Bottlenecks — and Why They're There

Each marker is not placed arbitrarily. Every one is grounded in a specific documented failure, a physical constraint, or a hydraulic limit confirmed by multiple data sources.

Plantage Middenlaan — Sewer Backup
Severity 97% — Critical
Critical
What happens here

Sewer backs up and surfaces through drain grates at the Artis main gate. Documented in Waternet's own incident database. Starts at rainfall above 15mm/hr.

Why this spot specifically

The sewer pipe here was sized in the 1970s. The street is 100% impervious — road, tram track, cycle lane, pavement. Every drop of rain goes straight into this one pipe. Above 15mm/hr it physically cannot handle the volume.

Plantage Kerklaan — Surface Runoff
Severity 91% — Critical
Critical
What happens here

Water sheets off the road and pools at the Kerklaan–Middenlaan intersection. Gutter capacity is overwhelmed. Confirmed by GOW data as impassable at 90mm.

Why this spot specifically

Highest impervious coverage in the district (~94%). No infiltration system, no buffer connection. Runoff has nowhere to go except downstream toward Muidergracht. This is why the 3rd filter proposal targets this street.

Hippo Bridge (Nijlpaardbrug) — Filter Inlet
Severity 86% — High
High
What happens here

Western entry point of the helophyte filter. Peak inflow documented at ~97m³/hr — near the design limit of the pre-treatment sand-filter beach.

Why this spot specifically

The bridge abutments and the narrow sand-filter beach create a physical bottleneck. When inflow exceeds capacity, the pre-treatment saturates — reducing biological filtration efficiency in the sections downstream.

Southeast Passage — Narrow Corridor
Severity 80% — Elevated
Elevated
What happens here

Only overflow route southward toward Sarphatistraat. Water rises faster here than anywhere else in the district when Muidergracht backs up.

Why this spot specifically

Roughly 4m of effective drainage width between the Artis boundary wall and adjacent buildings — serving a much larger upstream catchment. Geometrically impossible for water to escape laterally.

Railway Bridge (Spoorbrug) — Filter Outlet
Severity 74% — Elevated
Elevated
What happens here

Eastern outlet of the helophyte filter. Old bridge abutments restrict outflow width. Treated water returns to canal here — risk is filter performance, not street flooding.

Why lower severity than inlet

By this point water has passed through most of the filter. The bottleneck here reduces hydraulic gradient through the filter, slowing treatment — but doesn't cause street flooding the way the inlet and sewer points do.

Interventions

The Three Flood Solutions

The simulator models three distinct interventions. They are not interchangeable — each operates at a different scale, a different time, and solves a different part of the problem.

Large-Scale Treatment Infrastructure · Built along Entrepotdok
Helophyte Filter — Entrepotdok
A 368-metre constructed wetland along the south bank of Entrepotdok — from Hippo Bridge to the old Railway Bridge. Uses marsh plants (reed and galingale) as a biological treatment system. Water flows through sand and gravel colonised by microbes that break down pollutants: phosphorus, nitrates, heavy metals, E. coli. Three filter types run in sequence: vertical flow → horizontal flow → open surface wetland.

Underground Aquifer Storage · Inside Artis Zoo
BlueBloqs Rainwater Buffer
An underground circular water system by FieldFactors. A 5-step cycle: capture surface rainwater → retain in underground tank → purify through BlueBiofilter → store in the deep sandy aquifer 30 metres below ground → recover for reuse. The BlueWell bypasses Amsterdam's impermeable clay/peat surface by going straight into the Pleistocene sand aquifer beneath.

Why this matters: This is not a wadi. A classic wadi relies on water soaking into the ground — which doesn't happen in Amsterdam's clay-on-peat soil (infiltration rate: <1mm/hr). BlueBloqs bypasses the soil entirely. The storm event and the water release are decoupled — the system captures the peak instantly and releases it on its own schedule.

Design Logic

The Layered System — How It All Fits Together

The three interventions are not alternatives to each other. They operate at completely different time scales and storm intensities. Together they form a layered defence that covers the full spectrum of rain events.

Layer 1
Micro-wadis
Everyday rain (5–10mm events) — 80% of all rain events by count.
Small planted infiltration trenches, tree pits with engineered soil, rain gardens at street intersections. These handle the frequent, small events that build up sewer load over the year. They don't need to work during the extreme storm — they reduce the baseline load so the sewer has more capacity when the big event comes.
Layer 2
Helophyte Filter
Heavy events (20–80mm) — the peak storm routing and treatment layer.
The 4,000m² Entrepotdok filter intercepts the peak flow from the Muidergracht corridor, treats it biologically, and returns clean water to the canal at a controlled rate. It cuts peak flow by 45%. Crucially, it works regardless of soil permeability — water moves through the constructed medium, not into the ground.
Layer 3
BlueBloqs
Extreme events + dry summer periods — the circular storage layer.
Captures and stores whatever the filter can't handle. Decouples the storm from the release — the water goes underground during the event and comes back up for irrigation, toilet flushing, and zoo pond topping throughout the summer. One system solves both: too much water in winter, too little in summer.

None of these systems overlap. They each handle a different intensity of event, at a different time scale. The simulator's "Adaptive" mode activates all three simultaneously — which is why the water depth drops from 70cm to ~38cm in the extreme scenario.

Honest Assessment

What the Simulator Does Not Have

Every model has limits. These are the honest gaps — what would make the analysis stronger if it were available.

Missing: Water depth raster files (.tif)

The Waterdiepte_70mm and _90mm GeoTIFF files were absent from the dataset folder — only the coordinate (.tfw) and metadata (.xml) files remained. The hex grid weights were calibrated against the building and road risk data instead. The simulation is consistent with Waternet's risk patterns, but does not directly read pixel-level depth values.

Missing: Ground-truth infiltration map for Kerklaan and Middenlaan

The wadi discussion in this project is partly theoretical because we don't have a soil permeability survey for the specific parcels. There may be isolated zones near the Hortus Botanicus or recent construction sites where surface infiltration is actually viable. Without that data, you can't know where a planted infiltration trench would work vs. where it would just become a muddy pond. A geotechnical survey of the street-level soil would resolve this.

Missing: Exact installation coordinates for BlueBloqs

The BlueBloqs marker is placed at an approximate representative point inside Artis Zoo. The project documents confirm it's an Artis interior system but don't specify the installation location down to a building or enclosure. The marker should be treated as planning-level precision, not construction-level.

What would make this stronger

A soil permeability survey along Kerklaan and Middenlaan · Full access to the .tif raster files · Confirmed BlueBloqs installation coordinates · Real-time KNMI rainfall data for the Plantage weather station · A flow rate measurement at Hippo Bridge inlet during an actual storm event.
